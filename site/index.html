00The question

If a digital-trade policy moves, which of India's small businesses move with it?

India's small businesses increasingly produce and consume services that cross borders entirely online, software, design, back-office work, telecommunications, the licensing of foreign software. This study examines a single practical question: when the rules of digital trade shift, which firms are affected?

WTO services-trade data India MSME registrations 2005–2025
↓ Read top to bottom, each idea sets up the next chart

The argument, in brief.

These cross-border service flows depend on three things: digital platforms (an app store, a cloud provider, a freelancing marketplace), the internet infrastructure beneath them, and a thin layer of international trade rules. When any one of these shifts, a platform changes its fees, a country requires that data stay onshore, a long-standing customs truce on digital goods lapses, the firms in the affected sectors are exposed.

This study identifies, sector by sector, which Indian MSMEs are most exposed to those shifts. It does not ask whether digital trade is growing (it plainly is), nor whether India should deregulate (a question of advocacy). The question is narrower and more actionable: if a policy moves, which firms move with it?

Exposure has two components, both necessary

Exposure is defined here as the combination of two separate measurements. Either one alone is misleading.

Many firms but no digital trade, a local repair trade, for instance, is irrelevant to digital-trade policy. Heavy digital trade but few firms means few MSMEs stand to be affected. The sectors of interest are those where both are high, shown directly in the final chart.

01Key concepts

Concepts and terminology

The data originates from the WTO and India's MSME ministry, both of which rely heavily on classification acronyms. Five concepts underpin every chart that follows. Underlined terms throughout carry their definitions on hover.

A

The "digital channel" is one of four ways a service crosses a border

The WTO divides service trade into four modes of supply. Only the first, a service delivered over the wire, with no one travelling, is the digital channel relevant to this study. It is where platforms, data flows and digital-trade rules operate.

the digital channel

Mode 3 · presence
A local office
An Indian IT firm opens a subsidiary in Texas.
Mode 4 · people
A person travels
A consultant flies abroad for a three-month engagement.
B

EBOPS classifies what is being traded

It is the international standard for classifying services trade into categories, "Computer services", "Other business services", "Charges for the use of intellectual property", "Telecommunications", and so on. This study uses the eleven top-level categories; any row labelled by a service type follows EBOPS.

C

ISIC classifies who produces it, and a crosswalk joins the two

Trade data is organised by what is traded (EBOPS), but firm data is organised by what firms do, India registers its small businesses by industry code (NIC, which aligns with the international ISIC standard). Linking the two, counting the firms in the industries behind a given category of trade, requires a crosswalk that maps each trade category onto an industry section.

The crosswalk is not constructed for this study; it is taken from the WTO's own TiSMoS items-and-correspondence workbook. It is the single link between dollars of trade and counts of firms, which is what makes the exposure measure possible.

It is not always one-to-one. One EBOPS category, Other business services, maps to three industry sections at once. As shown later, this gives that category a structural advantage in the final score, noted explicitly rather than hidden.

D

The WTO publishes two overlapping datasets. Each is used where it is strongest: the newer one for the long trend line, the older one for category and mode detail. They reconcile to rounding error where they overlap, so the difference is immaterial.

02Act I

Is this trade real and growing?

Before mapping exposure, the scale of the trade must be established. India exports far more digital services than it imports, and the gap has widened sharply. The four figures below are the headline; the rest of this section explains them.

Digitally delivered services are services supplied across a border over the internet, Mode 1 by definition. Exports grew a steady ~5–7% a year through the 2010s, then rose to roughly 18% a year after the pandemic, pushing the net surplus past $200 billion.

04Act III

The second axis, India's small businesses

The trade side is now established. Exposure also requires the firm side: how many small businesses operate in each industry. This is a harder data problem than the trade side, and the limits of the public data are noted where they apply.

India's Udyam system has accumulated 57.7 million registrations. The government does not publish a full breakdown by industry and firm size. The most detailed public sector breakdown comes from a single document, Bulletin VII, current to September 2021. This study concentrates on the services side, where digitally tradeable activity is found.

Top divisions by firm count

Rolled up to industry section, the bridge to the score

Rolled up to ISIC sections, these divisions form the firm-count side of the exposure index. The sections that map to digital-service categories, information & communication, professional services, finance, admin support, are the ones the final score relies on.

+ Why the data stops at September 2021

MoMSME publishes a top-50 list of detailed industry codes in Bulletin VII, but no full industry-by-size cross-tab. The sector figures were therefore transcribed from that bulletin's tables and rolled up by industry section. The current 57.7 million total is the only post-2021 figure used, taken from the 2024-25 Annual Report.

The most-cited 2025 MSME digital-adoption survey covers manufacturing only, which is itself evidence of a measurement gap on the services side, part of the gap this study begins to address.

05Act IV

Putting the two axes together

The two-axis frame introduced at the outset, shown directly. Each point is one EBOPS category: its horizontal position is MSME scale (firm count), its vertical position is trade intensity (Mode 1 trade), each normalised to 0–1. The exposure score is the sum of the two, the distance toward the upper-right. Points nearest the upper-right corner are the most exposed.

The same eleven, as a ranking

What sits inside the top category

The highest-ranked row is not a single industry. Other business services (EBOPS SJXSJ34) maps to three ISIC sections at once, L, M and N: real estate; professional, scientific and technical activities; and administrative and support services. Its firm count is the sum across all three, the structural advantage flagged in the limitations below. Unpacking it shows what the rank actually rests on.

Admin & support and professional & scientific activities supply almost all of the firms; real estate is a small share. The genuinely cross-border, digitally-traded part is the professional cluster, management consultancy, accounting and tax, legal services, architecture and engineering, advertising. Much of the remainder, travel agencies, office and building support, vehicle leasing, fee-based real estate, is domestically oriented and never crosses a border over the wire.

One level deeper: which sub-sector is genuinely exposed

Bringing the trade side back in sharpens the result. Each activity has its own EBOPS trade sub-code with a Mode 1 figure, so the same two-axis method runs one level down, inside the category. Splitting each bar into its two halves makes the difference plain: a long indigo half is real cross-border trade; a long slate half is firm count alone.

→
The disaggregation sharpens the result, with one honest caveatLegal, accounting & management consulting is the genuinely exposed core, the largest cross-border Mode 1 trade in the category on a substantial firm base (a long indigo half); architecture & engineering follows. But the mixed "n.i.e." residual is no footnote: it holds about 559,000 of the category's 796,000 firms, so only roughly a fifth resolve cleanly into named, digitally-tradeable clusters, and it ranks high purely on that firm count (a long slate half, near-zero indigo). The finer cut sharpens the trade attribution, professional and technical consulting, even as most firms stay in an undifferentiated bucket.

What actually drove the surge

One last cut explains the rest. The category's digital trade splits into two children, professional and management consulting and a technical / other-business remainder, and only the first has moved. Consulting Mode 1 exports climbed from under $7 billion in 2005 to roughly $92 billion in 2022, a thirteen-fold rise, while the remainder little more than doubled and sits flat beside it.

So the headline ascent, Other business services overtaking Computer services, is almost entirely a consulting story. The lumped label, and even the eleven-category view, obscure it; the disaggregation makes it the clearest single finding here: India's celebrated "IT exports" are, increasingly, professional and management consulting delivered over the wire.

How the score is built

Each component is min-max normalised across the eleven rows to land in 0–1, then the two are added (range 0–2) and sorted. v0 deliberately uses two components only, two further axes (digital adoption, policy sensitivity) await data sources that meet a quality bar. The formula itself remains an open methodological question.

Four open questions about the score

The score is presented together with its principal objections. None are errors; each is a genuine modelling choice on which reasonable analysts disagree.

Question 1
Add, or multiply?

Adding lets a high firm-count compensate for near-zero trade. Tourism scores on firm count alone, even though its Mode 1 trade is ~0. Multiplying would zero it out. Which is right depends on what "exposure" means: how many firms could be affected (add) vs how much actually flows digitally (multiply).

Question 2
Adding unlike things

One component is a count of firms, the other is US dollars. Normalising obscures this, but 0.4-of-firms + 0.6-of-dollars is not 1.0 of any real quantity. This is why Transport ranks high on raw trade dollars that do not represent platform exposure.

Question 3
Equal weights?

v0 weights both components equally, a placeholder, not a finding. Defensible alternatives (variance-based, expert-assigned, sensitivity-based) all need data not yet pulled. Tuning weights without that data would be false precision.

Question 4
Which normalisation?

Min-max anchors the scale to the single biggest row, so Other business services hits 2.00 almost by construction. Other choices (z-score, rank-based) move the gaps between ranks, each with its own trade-off.

Known limitations

The crosswalk is not one-to-one: Other business services maps to three industry sections at once, giving it a built-in advantage on firm count. Two structurally different quantities (firm counts and dollars) are being summed. There is also a time mismatch, firm counts are Sep 2021, trade is 2022. The ranking is a starting point for analysis, not a conclusion.

06Scope

What this is not

Defining the limits of the analysis is part of its credibility. Five claims this study does not make:

Not causal
It doesn't prove digital trade causes anything

It identifies structural exposure, which sectors are most exposed, not cause and effect.

Not predictive
No model of what will happen

It indicates which sectors warrant attention if a policy moves, not what the outcome will be.

Not firm-level
No data on individual businesses

All figures are sector aggregates; individual firms cannot be isolated.

Not bilateral
India ↔ World only

Both WTO datasets report against "World" as the sole partner. No India-to-US or India-to-EU breakdown exists here.

Not advocacy
No policy recommendation

It is decision support, not a decision, a sharper starting point for a policymaker.

07Findings

What the data shows

Several findings reframe the usual account of "India's IT exports."

$200B+net surplus
India runs a ~$200 billion digital-services trade surplus that has decoupled from imports. Exports grew ~5–7% through the 2010s, then accelerated to ~18% a year after COVID.
#1category flip
"Other business services", consulting, R&D, professional work, has overtaken Computer services as the largest export category. "India's IT exports" is increasingly a misnomer for what's actually being traded.
~29×since 2005
Fees Indian firms pay to use foreign intellectual property and software have grown nearly thirtyfold to around $20 billion a year, the cleanest single dollar measure of how dependent the economy has become on foreign platforms.
2020the inflection
The Mode 1 share is rising in categories that didn't used to be tradeable digitally, health, education, consultancy. The pandemic was the turning point; telemedicine and remote services show up directly in the WTO data.
0tourism Mode 1
Tourism has essentially zero digital-channel trade, a tourist must travel. Yet it still ranks in the exposure score purely on firm count. It is the clearest illustration of why the add-versus-multiply question matters.
08Roadmap

What's deliberately left for a v2, and why

A v0 reports only what is verified and names what is not. Subsequent versions add components only once their data sources meet a quality threshold.

Deferred component
A digital-adoption axis

Whether firms in a sector actually use digital tools. The most prominent survey covers manufacturing only, unsuitable for a services index, so a services-inclusive source is being evaluated first.

Deferred component
A policy-sensitivity axis

How much existing or pending regulation touches each sector. This requires the OECD's Digital Services Trade Restrictiveness Index, planned next.

Method, v2
Fix the structural quirks

Split the three-section category fairly across its industries, and revisit add-versus-multiply with a sharper definition of what is exposed to what.

Data, v2
A fresher MSME cut

Replace the Sep 2021 sector snapshot if the ministry publishes a current industry-by-size table.
